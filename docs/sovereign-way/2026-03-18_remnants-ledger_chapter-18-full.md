### The Prussian Model

The American public school system was not designed to educate. It was designed to produce obedient citizens and efficient workers.

In the early nineteenth century, American education reformers—most notably Horace Mann—traveled to Prussia to study the Prussian education system. Prussia had developed a model of compulsory, state-funded education that was explicitly designed to produce three outcomes: obedient soldiers for the military, obedient workers for the factories, and obedient citizens for the state.

The Prussian model divided children by age into grades. It confined them to classrooms for fixed periods of time. It required them to sit in rows, face forward, raise their hands to speak, and move only when a bell rang. It taught them to follow instructions, memorize information, and regurgitate it on command. It punished independent thinking and rewarded compliance.

Mann and his fellow reformers imported this model to America. By the end of the nineteenth century, compulsory education laws had been enacted in every state. By the mid-twentieth century, the public school system had become the largest institution in America, consuming more of a child's waking hours than any other activity.

The system has been refined over the decades, but its fundamental architecture has not changed. The child enters at age five or six. For the next twelve to thirteen years, they spend approximately 16,000 hours in a state institution, being shaped by a curriculum designed by bureaucrats, taught by employees of the state, and evaluated by standardized tests that measure compliance, not wisdom.

By the time the child graduates, they have been thoroughly trained in the skills the Squatter Regime values: following instructions, meeting deadlines, accepting authority without question, and competing with their peers for the approval of institutional gatekeepers. They have not been trained in the skills the Covenant values: critical thinking, theological literacy, practical self-sufficiency, financial sovereignty, or the ability to govern themselves and their households.

### The Covenant School

The Renaissance Steward does not send their children to the Squatter Regime's training facility.

This is not a casual recommendation. This is a non-negotiable principle of the Sovereign Way. The education of your children is the single most important jurisdictional decision you will make as a parent. If you surrender your children to the De Facto education system for 16,000 hours, you should not be surprised when they emerge as De Facto citizens.

The Covenant School is the alternative. It may take several forms:

**Homeschooling** is the most direct form of Covenant Education. The parent—or a group of parents within the Bureau—takes full responsibility for the child's education. The curriculum is designed around the 81-Book Canon, the Divine Council worldview, practical skills, and the development of the whole person: spirit, mind, and body.

**The Bureau Academy** is a cooperative school operated by the Covenant Bureau. Multiple families pool their resources and their expertise to create a small school that serves the children of the Bureau. One parent teaches mathematics. Another teaches history (real history, not the Squatter Regime's version). Another teaches agriculture and animal husbandry. Another teaches theology and biblical languages. The children learn from adults who love them, in a community that knows them, according to a curriculum that prepares them for life in the Covenant ecosystem.

**The Apprenticeship Model** is the oldest and most effective form of education. Instead of confining the child to a classroom for twelve years, the apprenticeship model places the child alongside a skilled adult who teaches them a trade through direct, hands-on experience. The child learns carpentry by building. They learn farming by farming. They learn medicine by working alongside a healer. They learn theology by studying with a pastor.

The apprenticeship model produces adults who can actually do things—who can build a house, grow food, heal the sick, and govern a household. The public school model produces adults who can pass a standardized test and follow instructions. The difference is the difference between a Renaissance Steward and an oar-puller.

### The Seven Liberal Arts

The classical tradition of education—the tradition that produced the greatest minds in Western civilization—was built on the **Seven Liberal Arts**: the Trivium (Grammar, Logic, Rhetoric) and the Quadrivium (Arithmetic, Geometry, Music, Astronomy).

The Trivium teaches the child how to learn. Grammar teaches them the structure of language—how to read, write, and communicate with precision. Logic teaches them how to think—how to identify fallacies, construct arguments, and evaluate evidence. Rhetoric teaches them how to persuade—how to communicate truth effectively and beautifully.

The Quadrivium teaches the child the structure of creation. Arithmetic reveals the numerical order of the universe. Geometry reveals its spatial order. Music reveals its harmonic order. Astronomy reveals its celestial order.

Together, the Seven Liberal Arts produce a human being who can think clearly, communicate effectively, and perceive the order that the Creator embedded in the fabric of reality. This is the opposite of what the public school system produces, which is a human being who can follow instructions and consume products.

The Covenant School recovers the Seven Liberal Arts and places them within the theological framework of the 81-Book Canon. The child does not learn arithmetic as an abstract exercise; they learn it as a window into the mathematical mind of God. They do not learn astronomy as the study of random celestial mechanics; they learn it as the study of the luminaries that God placed in the firmament to mark His appointed times.

### The Discipleship Model

Ultimately, Covenant Education is not about curriculum. It is about discipleship.

The word "disciple" means "learner." In the biblical model, education is not the transfer of information from a textbook to a brain. It is the formation of a whole person through relationship with a mature mentor.

Jesus did not hand His disciples a textbook. He walked with them. He ate with them. He showed them how to pray, how to serve, how to confront injustice, how to love the broken, and how to stand before the powers of the world without flinching. He taught them by example, by parable, by direct instruction, and by sending them out to practice what they had learned.

The Covenant School follows this model. The teacher is not a credentialed professional delivering a standardized curriculum. The teacher is a mature covenant partner who opens their life to the student and says, "Watch me. Walk with me. Learn from my successes and my failures. And then go and do likewise."

This is how the Renaissance Steward is formed. Not in a classroom. Not by a textbook. Not by a standardized test. But by the slow, patient, relational process of one generation pouring itself into the next.

*"Impress them on your children. Talk about them when you sit at home and when you walk along the road, when you lie down and when you get up."*

Education is not a twelve-year program. It is a way of life. And it begins the moment you decide that your children will not be trained by the Squatter Regime.
