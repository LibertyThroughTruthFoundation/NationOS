### The Septuagint (LXX)
The Greek translation of the Hebrew Scriptures, completed in Alexandria, Egypt, between the 3rd and 2nd centuries BC. According to historical tradition (recorded in the *Letter of Aristeas*), it was translated by 72 Jewish scholars at the request of King Ptolemy II Philadelphus. 

The Septuagint was the primary Bible of the early Christian church. When Jesus and the Apostles quoted the Old Testament, they overwhelmingly quoted from the Septuagint. It contains the broader canon of books (including the Apocrypha) and preserves ancient chronological and theological readings that were later altered in the Masoretic Text. 

**The Evidence of the DSS:** For centuries, Western scholars dismissed the Septuagint as a "loose translation" whenever it differed from the Hebrew Masoretic Text. However, the discovery of the Dead Sea Scrolls at Qumran vindicated the Septuagint, revealing ancient Hebrew manuscripts that matched the LXX word-for-word against the Masoretic, proving the LXX was translated from an older, more accurate Hebrew source.

### The Masoretic Text (MT) and the Council of Jamnia Redaction
The Hebrew text of the Old Testament used as the basis for almost all modern Western Bibles (including the King James Version). It was compiled and standardized by a group of Jewish scribes known as the Masoretes between the 7th and 10th centuries AD. 

**The Redaction:** Following the destruction of the Temple in AD 70, rabbinic Judaism faced an existential crisis: the rise of Christianity, which was using the Greek Septuagint to prove that Jesus was the Messiah. In response, a rabbinic council (traditionally associated with Jamnia/Yavneh around AD 90) began the process of closing the Hebrew canon, deliberately excluding books that supported Christian theology or the Divine Council worldview (such as Enoch and Jubilees). The later Masoretes further altered specific prophecies and chronologies to obscure messianic fulfillment. 

**De Facto Application:** The 66-book Masoretic-based Bible is a redacted document. While still containing the word of God, it has been deliberately truncated by those who rejected the Messiah, leaving modern Western believers vulnerable to squatter theologies.

### The Dead Sea Scrolls (DSS) and the Qumran Community
A massive collection of ancient Jewish manuscripts discovered between 1947 and 1956 in the Qumran caves near the Dead Sea. Hidden by an ascetic Jewish sect (widely believed to be the Essenes) prior to the AD 70 destruction of Jerusalem, the scrolls date from the 3rd century BC to the 1st century AD.

The DSS represent the greatest archaeological vindication of the broader canon. The Qumran library contained multiple copies of the Book of Enoch (written in Aramaic) and the Book of Jubilees, proving these texts were considered highly authoritative, if not fully canonical, by Second Temple Jews. The community separated from the Jerusalem temple establishment precisely because they believed the temple priesthood had become corrupted and had abandoned the true solar calendar (as outlined in Jubilees and Enoch).

### The Book of Jubilees
Often called the "Little Genesis," Jubilees is an expansion and retelling of the events from Genesis and Exodus, structured around a strict chronological system of "jubilees" (periods of 49 years). It is fully canonical in the Ethiopian Orthodox Church and was found in multiple copies among the Dead Sea Scrolls.

Jubilees provides crucial details omitted from the redacted Genesis account, including the creation of angels on the first day, the specific names of the wives of the patriarchs, and the introduction of the 364-day solar calendar.

### The Books of Meqabyan (Ethiopian Maccabees)
Three books unique to the Ethiopian Orthodox 81-Book Canon. *They are completely distinct from the Greek Books of Maccabees (1-4 Maccabees) found in the Catholic and Eastern Orthodox canons.* 

While the Greek Maccabees are historical accounts of the Hasmonean revolt against Antiochus Epiphanes, the Ethiopian Meqabyan books are deeply philosophical and allegorical texts. The First Book of Meqabyan tells the story of a man named Meqabis and his sons who choose martyrdom over idolatry. The subsequent books deal with profound themes of pride, repentance, the resurrection of the dead, and the ultimate justice of God.

### Fetha Nagast (The Law of the Kings)
A theocratic legal code compiled in Arabic around 1240 AD by the Coptic Egyptian Christian writer Abu'l-Fada'il ibn al-Assal, and later translated into Ge'ez (the liturgical language of Ethiopia) in the 15th century. 

The *Fetha Nagast* is the governing text for applying the 81-Book Canon to civil, ecclesiastical, and communal life. It is divided into two parts: the first deals with Church law, hierarchy, and sacraments; the second deals with secular law, including family law, property rights, administration of justice, and the duties of kings.
