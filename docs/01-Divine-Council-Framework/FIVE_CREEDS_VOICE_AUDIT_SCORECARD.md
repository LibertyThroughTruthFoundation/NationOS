## IV. DETAILED VOICE PROFILES

### 1. Joel Webbon / Right Response Ministries (75% — Closest Ally)

Joel Webbon is the pastor of Covenant Bible Church in Austin, Texas, and the founder of Right Response Ministries. He represents the **closest single-voice alignment** with NationOS across the five creeds, scoring above 70% on four of the five pillars. His February 2025 episode "Zionism, Modern Israel, and The Divine Council" with Andrew Isker demonstrates an emerging engagement with Divine Council theology, though it remains unclear whether he has achieved the depth of a full Deut 32:8 LXX/DSS cosmic geography framework. His supersessionism is robust — he has explicitly identified the Church as the true Israel of God and rejected Zionism. His patriarchal household theology is strong, and his collaboration with Stephen Wolfe on "What Is A Nation?" demonstrates a sophisticated ethnos theology. His postmillennialism is partial preterist, not full preterist, which creates a gap on Creed 4.

**Primary gap:** Divine Council depth. Webbon engages the concept but may not have integrated the Enoch-Jubilees layer or the cosmic geography of Deut 32:8 at the level NationOS requires.

**Strategic value:** Webbon's audience (Christian nationalist, Reformed, patriarchal) is the **exact demographic** NationOS is designed to serve. His platform could be a distribution channel for NationOS content, and his theological gaps are precisely what NationOS fills.

### 2. Kendall Lankford / The Shepherd's Church / The PRODCAST (68% — Strong Ally)

Kendall Lankford is a pastor in the CREC (Communion of Reformed Evangelical Churches) and the host of The PRODCAST. His four-part series "The Case for a Preterist Supersessionism" (November 2025) is the **single most NationOS-aligned theological document** discovered outside of the project itself. His explicit combination of preterism and supersessionism — arguing that the ingrafting of Romans 11 was a first-century reality, that AD 70 sealed the Old Covenant forever, and that the Church is the true Israel — places him at 95% on Creed 2 and 90% on Creed 4. His CREC affiliation provides strong patriarchal household theology (his next article is "Embracing Biblical Patriarchy in the Home"), scoring 80% on Creed 3.

**Primary gap:** Divine Council and Babel Mišpāṭ. Lankford's framework is thoroughly Reformed and covenantal, but it does not appear to engage the supernatural worldview of the Divine Council or the ethnos theology of nations and boundaries. The CREC has mixed positions on Christian nationalism, and Lankford's focus is eschatological rather than political.

**Strategic value:** Lankford's preterist supersessionism series could serve as a **companion text** to NationOS Creed 2 and Creed 4 documents. His audience (CREC, Reformed, eschatology-focused) overlaps with but does not duplicate Webbon's audience.

### 3. Doug Wilson / Christ Church Moscow / Canon Press (66% — Structural Ally)

Doug Wilson is the founder of Christ Church in Moscow, Idaho, and the patriarch of the CREC denomination. He is a prolific author and the most prominent voice in the Reformed Christian nationalism movement. His household theology and patriarchal framework are among the strongest in the landscape (90% on Creed 3), and his supersessionism is robust (85% on Creed 2). His postmillennialism provides partial preterist alignment (65% on Creed 4), and his vision of "a global order of Christian nations" demonstrates strong nations theology (75% on Creed 5).

**Primary gap:** Divine Council. Wilson is a classical Reformed theologian who does not engage the Heiser-level supernatural worldview, the Enoch-Jubilees literature, or the cosmic geography of Deut 32:8. His framework is thoroughly Augustinian and Calvinist, not Second Temple Jewish.

**Strategic value:** Wilson's Canon Press publishing ecosystem and CREC denominational network represent the **largest institutional infrastructure** in the Reformed Christian nationalism space. NationOS could position itself as the theological depth layer that Wilson's movement lacks.

### 4. Michael Heiser (46% — Divine Council Foundation)

Michael Heiser (1963-2023) remains the **foundational voice** for Creed 1. His work on the Divine Council — particularly *The Unseen Realm* (2015) and *Supernatural* (2015) — established the academic and popular framework for understanding Deuteronomy 32:8 in its LXX/Dead Sea Scrolls context, Psalm 82 as a judgment on the bene elohim, and the cosmic geography of nations allotted to lesser elohim. No other voice has achieved this level of Divine Council scholarship in accessible form.

However, Heiser's alignment collapses on the remaining four creeds. He was **not** a supersessionist, leaning toward a future mass conversion of ethnic Israel. He had **no** ordered loves or household theology framework. He was **not** a preterist, adopting an eclectic amillennial position while dismissing eschatological debates as unproductive. And while his Deut 32:8 framework provides the theological architecture for Babel Mišpāṭ, he **never** applied it to modern ethnos theology, immigration, or gates. Bryan also notes that Heiser gets biblical cosmology wrong, accepting conventional cosmology rather than the cosmological framework described in the texts he studied.

**Strategic value:** Heiser is the **foundation stone** for Creed 1, not a building. NationOS takes his Divine Council framework and builds upon it what he never did — integrating it with supersessionism, preterism, ordered loves, and ethnos theology.

### 5. The Bible Project (25% — Pedagogical Resource)

Tim Mackie and Jon Collins created the most visually compelling biblical theology content on the internet. Their engagement with spiritual beings, the cosmic temple, and the Divine Council narrative scores a moderate 55% on Creed 1 — they teach the concepts but sanitize them for a mainstream audience, avoiding the Enoch-Jubilees layer and the more confrontational implications. Their explicit avoidance of supersessionism (their episode "Does the Church Supersede Israel?" with Andrew Rillera concludes with a "both/and" framework), their egalitarian household theology ("Equality in the Household" class reframes Paul's household codes as "reimagined"), and their avoidance of strong eschatological positions place them at 25% or below on Creeds 2-5.

**Strategic value:** The Bible Project's **visual pedagogy** is world-class. NationOS could learn from their production methods while providing the theological substance they avoid.

### 6. The God Culture (35% — Niche Geography)

Timothy Jay Schwab's The God Culture focuses on Lost Tribes of Israel migration patterns, the identification of Ophir with the Philippines, and biblical geography. While this provides interesting alignment on nations and boundaries (60% on Creed 5), the channel's Lost Tribes theology may actually be **anti-supersessionist** (ethnic Israel still matters as a distinct entity), and concerns about anti-Trinitarian positions have been raised by critics. The channel does not engage Divine Council theology at the Heiser level, does not articulate preterist eschatology, and does not provide household theology.

**Strategic value:** Limited. The God Culture's geographic research may provide supplementary data for NationOS ethnos theology, but the theological framework diverges significantly.
