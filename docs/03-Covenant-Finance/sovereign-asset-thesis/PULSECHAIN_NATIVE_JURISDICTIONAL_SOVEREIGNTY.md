## I. The Core Thesis

There is a persistent assumption within the sovereignty movement that digital assets require a legal wrapper — a trust, a private membership association, a 508(c)(1)(A) foundation, or some other entity structure — in order to be "protected" from regulatory capture. This assumption is understandable. It comes from decades of operating within a system where every asset, every transaction, and every relationship is mediated by a legal entity that can be subpoenaed, regulated, taxed, or seized.

The thesis of this document is that **PulseChain-native digital assets are already jurisdictionally sovereign by design**, and that the legal, technical, and precedential evidence now confirms this beyond reasonable dispute. The 508(c)(1)(A) foundation, the trust structure, the private membership association — these are essential tools for the **fiat interface**, for real property, for employment relationships, and for the banking layer. They are not necessary for the digital assets themselves. The assets are already free. The question is whether the holder understands this and acts accordingly.

This is not a speculative legal theory. It is a conclusion supported by three independent lines of evidence: the technical architecture of PulseChain, the federal court ruling in *SEC v. Schueler*, and the emerging global legal consensus on digital assets as a distinct category of property.

## II. The Technical Foundation — Why PulseChain Assets Have No Jurisdictional Anchor

Traditional jurisdiction requires three elements: a **person** who can be served, a **place** where the activity occurs, and a **transaction** that connects the activity to the court's authority. PulseChain-native assets systematically eliminate all three.

### 2.1 No Issuer (No Person)

The most fundamental property of a PulseChain-native asset is that it has **no issuer**. When PulseChain launched on May 10, 2023, it executed a full-state fork of the Ethereum blockchain. Every address, every token balance, every smart contract was copied in a single, irreversible genesis event. The result is that assets like pDAI, pWBTC, pUSDT, and pUSDC exist on PulseChain not because someone created them, but because they were **inherited** from Ethereum's state at a specific block height.

No individual minted pDAI. No company issued pWBTC. No foundation deployed pUSDT on PulseChain. These assets exist because the chain exists. They have no origin story that involves a securities offering, a capital raise, or a contractual relationship between an issuer and a buyer. This is a critical legal distinction. As the Harvard Law School Forum on Corporate Governance has argued, the cryptoasset itself is never a security — the **investment contract** (the arrangement of sale between an issuer and a buyer) may be, but the asset is not. [1] When there is no issuer and no investment contract, there is no securities nexus. The asset is simply property.

### 2.2 No Geographic Location (No Place)

PulseChain is a decentralized proof-of-stake blockchain with validators distributed across the globe. There is no central server, no corporate headquarters, no data center that constitutes a "place" for jurisdictional purposes. The network exists as a distributed state machine — a shared computational reality maintained by consensus among independent nodes.

When a holder possesses pWBTC in a self-custody wallet, the asset does not "reside" in any jurisdiction. It exists as an entry in a distributed ledger that is simultaneously replicated across every full node on the network. The holder's private key — the cryptographic proof of control — may be stored on a hardware device in Mexico, a software wallet on a phone in the United States, or a seed phrase written on paper in a Swiss vault. The asset itself has no location. It is, in the language of property law, a **res nullius** — a thing belonging to no jurisdiction until a specific legal claim is asserted against a specific person in a specific place. [2]

### 2.3 No Intermediary (No Transaction Within Reach)

PulseChain transactions are peer-to-peer. When one holder sends pDAI to another, the transaction is broadcast to the network, validated by distributed validators, and recorded on the blockchain. There is no bank, no exchange, no clearinghouse, and no custodian that mediates the transfer. The transaction occurs between two cryptographic addresses — not between two legal persons in a court's jurisdiction.

This is the property that makes PulseChain-native assets fundamentally different from assets held on centralized exchanges (which can be subpoenaed), in bank accounts (which can be frozen), or in trust structures (which can be pierced). The peer-to-peer transaction has no intermediary that a court can compel to act. The only way to seize a self-custodied PulseChain asset is to obtain the private key — which requires either the holder's cooperation or physical access to the storage medium.

| Jurisdictional Element | Traditional Asset | PulseChain-Native Asset |
|---|---|---|
| **Person (Issuer)** | Company, bank, or government that issued the asset | None — asset inherited from Ethereum state fork |
| **Place (Location)** | Registered in a specific jurisdiction; held at a specific institution | No location — distributed across global validator network |
| **Transaction (Nexus)** | Mediated by banks, exchanges, or custodians within court's reach | Peer-to-peer; no intermediary to subpoena or compel |
| **Seizure Mechanism** | Court order to custodian, bank, or exchange | Requires physical possession of private key |

## III. The Legal Precedent — *SEC v. Schueler* and the Jurisdictional Failure

The technical argument is compelling on its own merits. But the legal precedent now confirms it. On February 28, 2025, U.S. District Judge Carol Bagley Amon of the Eastern District of New York dismissed **every claim** the Securities and Exchange Commission had brought against Richard Heart (Richard J. Schueler), HEX, PulseChain, and PulseX. [3] The SEC subsequently confirmed on April 21, 2025, that it would not amend or refile its complaint, allowing the dismissal to stand with **no findings of wrongdoing**. [4]

The significance of this ruling cannot be overstated. The SEC is the most powerful financial regulator on earth. It brought the full weight of its enforcement apparatus against PulseChain and its associated assets. And it lost — not on the merits of whether the assets were securities, but on the **threshold question of jurisdiction itself**.

### 3.1 The Court's Reasoning

Judge Amon's ruling addressed the jurisdictional question directly. The court found that:

> "The relevant online communications described in the Complaint during the offer periods consist of untargeted, globally available information. The SEC failed to plead sufficient facts to suggest that Heart's online statements were purposefully directed to the United States rather than a global audience." [3]

The court further ruled that the mere participation of U.S. persons in the offerings did not establish jurisdiction, stating that the complaint "merely alleges that an unspecified number of U.S.-based investors participated in the offerings" without demonstrating that transactions occurred within the United States. [3]

The court also dismissed the SEC's fraud claims in full, finding that the agency had failed to demonstrate that any investor communications were misleading or that the alleged misuse of funds occurred within U.S. jurisdiction. [4]

### 3.2 What the Ruling Means for PulseChain-Native Assets

The implications extend far beyond Richard Heart personally. The ruling establishes that:

**First**, assets on a globally distributed blockchain network do not automatically fall within U.S. regulatory jurisdiction simply because U.S. persons hold them. The SEC must demonstrate a specific, intentional nexus to the U.S. market — and for decentralized, globally available assets, this nexus does not exist.

**Second**, the tokens themselves — HEX, PulseChain (PLS), and PulseX (PLSX) — now carry what legal commentators have described as "a level of legal clarity few other tokens enjoy." [4] The SEC's decision not to appeal or amend means that the dismissal stands as precedent. These assets have been tested by the most aggressive regulator in the world and have emerged with their non-security status confirmed.

**Third**, the ruling reinforces that "publishing software — especially open-source blockchain code — is protected speech." [4] PulseChain is open-source software. Its existence is an act of publication, not an act of securities issuance. The assets that exist on it are products of that software's execution, not products of a capital-raising scheme.

### 3.3 The Fork Assets — An Even Stronger Case

If the court could not establish jurisdiction over HEX, PulseChain, and PulseX — assets that were associated with specific sacrifice phases and a known founder — then the jurisdictional case against **fork assets** like pDAI, pWBTC, pUSDT, and pUSDC is even weaker. These assets have no sacrifice phase, no founder association, no marketing campaign, and no capital-raising event of any kind. They are copies of Ethereum-state assets that came into existence through the mechanical operation of a blockchain fork. There is literally no person, no place, and no transaction that connects them to any regulatory jurisdiction.

## IV. The Global Legal Consensus — Digital Assets as a Third Category of Property

The jurisdictional sovereignty of PulseChain-native assets is further reinforced by the emerging global legal consensus that digital assets constitute a **distinct category of property** — neither tangible goods nor mere contractual rights, but a third kind of thing that is recognized and protected by law.

### 4.1 United Kingdom — The Property (Digital Assets etc) Bill

The United Kingdom's Property (Digital Assets etc) Bill, which completed its House of Lords stages in May 2025 and is set for Royal Assent, explicitly recognizes certain digital assets as a **third category of personal property** under England and Wales law. [5] This legislation builds on the landmark case *AA v Persons Unknown* (2019), in which the High Court treated Bitcoin as property eligible for proprietary injunctions, and *Tulip Trading Ltd v Bitcoin Association* (2022), which explored the duties owed to private key holders. [5]

The UK's approach is significant because it recognizes that digital assets are not merely data entries or contractual claims — they are **property** in the full legal sense, with all the protections that property status entails: the right to exclude others, the right to transfer, and the right to legal remedy in case of theft or fraud.

### 4.2 United States — Property for Tax, Not Securities for Regulation

In the United States, the Internal Revenue Service has treated digital assets as **property** for tax purposes since 2014. [6] This classification means that digital assets are subject to capital gains tax when sold, but it also means they carry the legal attributes of property: they can be owned, transferred, inherited, and — critically — they are protected by the Fourth and Fifth Amendments against unreasonable search and seizure and compelled self-incrimination.

The *SEC v. Schueler* ruling reinforces this property classification by demonstrating that the SEC's attempt to classify PulseChain-associated assets as securities failed at the jurisdictional threshold. The assets are property. They are not securities. And the federal government's own enforcement arm has been unable to establish otherwise.

### 4.3 European Union — MiCA and the Classification Framework

The European Union's Markets in Crypto-Assets Regulation (MiCA), fully applicable since December 2024, provides a comprehensive classification framework for crypto-assets but does not redefine property law. [7] MiCA classifies crypto-assets into categories (e-money tokens, asset-referenced tokens, and other crypto-assets) and imposes regulatory requirements on **issuers** and **service providers** — not on holders of self-custodied assets. The regulatory burden falls on the intermediaries, not on the assets themselves or their holders.

This is consistent with the broader principle: regulation attaches to **persons and institutions**, not to the assets. A self-custodied PulseChain-native asset, held in a non-custodial wallet with no intermediary, falls outside the regulatory perimeter of MiCA just as it falls outside the jurisdictional reach of the SEC.

| Jurisdiction | Legal Status of Digital Assets | Key Precedent or Legislation |
|---|---|---|
| **United States** | Property (IRS since 2014); not securities (*SEC v. Schueler*) | *SEC v. Schueler* dismissed Feb 28, 2025; SEC declined to amend Apr 21, 2025 |
| **United Kingdom** | Third category of personal property | Property (Digital Assets etc) Bill (2025); *AA v Persons Unknown* (2019) |
| **European Union** | Regulated at issuer/service provider level; not at holder level | MiCA (fully applicable Dec 2024); ESMA guidelines (Mar 2025) |
| **Australia** | Property capable of being held in trust | *Ruscoe v Cryptopia* (2020) — crypto recognized as property |

## V. The Distinction — What the 508(c)(1)(A) and Trust Structures Actually Protect

If PulseChain-native assets are already jurisdictionally sovereign, what is the purpose of the 508(c)(1)(A) foundation, the trust structure, and the private membership association?

The answer is precise: **these structures protect the fiat interface, not the digital assets.**

### 5.1 The Fiat Interface

The moment a sovereign digital asset touches the traditional financial system, it enters a jurisdiction. Converting pDAI to USD requires a bank account. A bank account requires an entity — a person, a corporation, a foundation, or a trust. That entity is subject to the laws of the jurisdiction where the bank operates. The 508(c)(1)(A) foundation provides the most favorable entity structure for this interface: automatic tax exemption, no filing requirement, and privacy protections that shield the foundation's internal operations from public disclosure.

### 5.2 Real Property

Real estate exists in a specific jurisdiction and is subject to that jurisdiction's property laws. In Mexico's restricted zone, foreign ownership requires a *fideicomiso* (bank trust). In the United States, real property is subject to state property tax, zoning laws, and eminent domain. The trust structure and the foundation protect real property holdings by creating a legal entity that can hold title, receive income, and shield the beneficial owner from direct liability.

### 5.3 Employment and Income

A salary paid from a foundation to an individual is a transaction between two legal persons within a specific jurisdiction. The 508(c)(1)(A) foundation provides the tax-exempt entity that can pay this salary. The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) provides the individual tax benefit for the recipient. These are fiat-world mechanisms for fiat-world transactions.

### 5.4 The Digital Assets — Already Sovereign

The digital assets themselves — pTGC, pDAI, pWBTC, HEX, PLS, PLSX, BEAR — do not need any of these wrappers. They exist on a decentralized network. They have no issuer. They have no geographic location. They are transferred peer-to-peer without intermediaries. They have been tested by the SEC and found to be outside its jurisdictional reach. They are recognized as property by multiple legal systems. They are controlled exclusively by the holder's private key.

The 508(c)(1)(A) foundation is the **bridge** between the sovereign digital world and the regulated fiat world. It is not a **shield** for the digital assets. The digital assets are their own shield.

| Layer | What It Protects | Why It Needs a Legal Wrapper |
|---|---|---|
| **Digital Assets** (pTGC, pDAI, pWBTC, HEX, PLS) | Self-custodied tokens on PulseChain | **Does not need a wrapper** — already jurisdictionally sovereign |
| **Fiat Conversion** (USD, MXN) | Bank accounts, off-ramp transactions | Requires an entity (508(c)(1)(A), LLC, trust) for banking access |
| **Real Property** (house, land) | Physical assets in a specific jurisdiction | Requires a *fideicomiso* or trust for foreign ownership; subject to local property law |
| **Employment Income** (salary) | Payments from foundation to individual | Requires a tax-exempt entity and FEIE qualification for tax efficiency |
| **Governance** (DAO, PMA) | Community decision-making structures | May require legal recognition depending on jurisdiction and activity |

## VII. Practical Implications for the Sovereign Stewardship Audit

The native jurisdictional sovereignty thesis has direct implications for how the Sovereign Stewardship Audit is conducted and what recommendations are made to clients.

### 7.1 Self-Custody Is Non-Negotiable

The sovereignty of PulseChain-native assets depends entirely on **self-custody**. The moment an asset is deposited on a centralized exchange, it enters the exchange's jurisdiction — and the exchange is subject to the laws of the jurisdiction where it operates. The Audit must emphasize that self-custody (hardware wallet, seed phrase backup, multi-signature where appropriate) is the foundational requirement for jurisdictional sovereignty. Without self-custody, the thesis does not apply.

### 7.2 The Foundation Protects the Bridge, Not the Treasury

Clients who are establishing or operating a 508(c)(1)(A) foundation should understand that the foundation's primary function is to provide a compliant interface with the fiat financial system. The foundation holds the bank account, pays the salary, receives donations, and manages real property. The digital assets in the covenant treasury do not need to be "held by" the foundation in any legal sense — they are held by the private key, which may be controlled by the foundation's steward but exists independently of any legal entity.

### 7.3 The Risk Is at the Interface, Not in the Treasury

The genuine risks for covenant economy participants are concentrated at the **fiat interface**: the off-ramp from crypto to fiat, the bank account, the real property transaction, the employment relationship. These are the points where traditional jurisdiction applies and where proper legal structures (foundation, trust, *fideicomiso*) are essential. The digital treasury itself — properly self-custodied on PulseChain — is the most secure layer of the architecture.

### 7.4 Operational Security Is the New Legal Compliance

In the sovereign digital world, **operational security replaces legal compliance** as the primary protection mechanism. The holder who loses their private key loses their assets — no court can restore them. The holder who exposes their seed phrase to a phishing attack loses their assets — no insurance covers them. The Audit must include a thorough assessment of the client's operational security posture: hardware wallet usage, seed phrase storage, multi-signature configuration, and social engineering awareness.

## VIII. Conclusion — The Assets Are Already Free

The argument of this document is not that PulseChain-native assets *should be* jurisdictionally sovereign. It is that they **already are**. The technical architecture eliminates the jurisdictional anchors (no issuer, no location, no intermediary). The federal court precedent confirms that the SEC cannot establish jurisdiction over these assets. The global legal consensus recognizes digital assets as a distinct category of property controlled by the private key holder. And the theological framework identifies this sovereignty as a design property of the covenant economy.

The 508(c)(1)(A) foundation, the trust structure, and the private membership association remain essential tools — but for the fiat interface, not for the digital treasury. The practitioner who understands this distinction can advise clients with precision: use the legal structures where they are needed (banking, real property, employment), and trust the technical architecture where it is sufficient (digital asset custody, peer-to-peer transfer, covenant treasury management).

The assets are already free. The question is whether the holder knows it.
