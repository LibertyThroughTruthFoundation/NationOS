### **2. Point-by-Point Rebuttal Script**

Here is the full response, structured for clarity and impact. This can be posted as a long-form reply or broken into a thread.

**Part 1: The Foundational Question of Authority**

> Hey Bill, appreciate the directness. Let's get to the core of it. Your entire argument rests on a critical choice of authority. You have chosen to subordinate the inspired text of the New Testament to an extra-biblical Jewish tradition. That is the fundamental point of disagreement.

**Part 2: Refuting the "Luke was Mistaken" Claim**

> You claim Luke was "mistaken" for including Cainan (Luke 3:36). Let's be very clear about what that means. You are claiming that the Holy Spirit, who inspired the scripture (2 Tim 3:16), made a genealogical error.
>
> This is a dangerous position. It elevates modern textual criticism above the inerrant apostolic witness. The alternative is far more biblically sound: **Luke was not mistaken. He was correct.**
>
> Luke's genealogy, including Cainan, perfectly matches the Septuagint (LXX) text of Genesis 11. The apostles and NT authors quoted the LXX approximately 90% of the time. They treated it as scripture. The DSS confirm that the Hebrew texts of the Second Temple period were diverse; the MT was a later, standardized text created by the Masoretes *after* the time of Christ.
>
> So the question is not whether Luke made an error. The question is: **Who do we trust more? The apostle Luke and the LXX text he used, or the post-70 AD Masoretes who had a clear motive to redact the timeline?** I will stand with the apostolic witness every time.

**Part 3: Dismantling the "Millennial Day Calendar" Argument**

> Your second pillar is the "millennial day calendar" from Tanna Debe Eliyahu and the Epistle of Barnabas, which you say the LXX violates. This is a classic case of inverting the order of authority.
>
> You are using a non-canonical, extra-biblical tradition as a measuring stick to invalidate the chronology found in the very scriptures the apostles used (the LXX). This is eisegesis. It is forcing a man-made theological *system* onto the Bible, and then editing the Bible to fit the system.
>
> The Bible does not explicitly lay out a 6000-year timeline. That is an interpretation, a tradition. The LXX, on the other hand, provides an explicit, internally consistent chronology that was accepted by the NT authors. To reject the LXX *because* it contradicts a non-canonical tradition is to make the tradition your functional authority, not the scripture itself.
>
> The "millennial day calendar" is the scribal error here, not the LXX's timeline. It is a tradition of men being used to nullify the Word of God as delivered by the apostles.

**Part 4: Addressing the Argument from Incredulity**

> Your final point is that it's unbelievable men would wait an extra 100 years to have children. This is an argument from personal incredulity, not scripture. You are projecting modern sensibilities onto ancient patriarchs who lived for hundreds of years.
>
> In a world where men lived 400, 500, or 900 years, the difference between begetting a son at 35 (MT) versus 135 (LXX) is not the biological absurdity it seems to us. It is a weak, subjective argument that carries no textual weight against the manuscript evidence.

**Part 5: The Conclusion — The Weight of Apostolic Evidence**

> Bill, the weight of the evidence is decisively **for** the LXX, not against it. The case is simple:
>
> 1.  **The Apostles Used It:** The NT authors overwhelmingly quoted the LXX.
> 2.  **Luke Confirmed It:** Luke's inspired genealogy matches the LXX, not the MT.
> 3.  **The DSS Vindicated It:** The Dead Sea Scrolls proved the MT was not the sole "original," but one of several text types, and that the LXX often preserves an older reading.
>
> To defend the MT's shorter timeline, you are forced to claim the apostle Luke was mistaken and that an extra-biblical tradition trumps the scriptures the apostles used. That is too high a price to pay. I will build my theology on the foundation of the apostles and prophets, with Christ as the cornerstone — and that foundation points directly to the LXX as the most reliable witness to the Old Testament text.
