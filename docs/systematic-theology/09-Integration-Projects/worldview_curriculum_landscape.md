## 3. Detailed Analysis: The Four Major Players

### R.C. Sproul & Ligonier Ministries: The Professor in the Lecture Hall

Ligonier's offerings are the gold standard for academic, Reformed theological education. Their series are not a single, unified worldview curriculum, but a library of distinct courses:

*   **"Dust to Glory" (57 parts):** A masterful, comprehensive survey of the entire Bible. Its goal is biblical literacy, not worldview construction.
*   **"Christian Worldview" (12 parts):** A comparative analysis of competing philosophies (Secularism, Humanism, etc.). It is an excellent intellectual defense of Christianity, but it is not designed to create a holistic, lived experience.
*   **"Defending Your Faith" (32 parts):** A deep dive into classical apologetics. It equips the mind, but it is not a full-orbed discipleship tool.

**Conclusion:** Ligonier provides the best academic content within the 66-book framework. We can borrow its commitment to theological depth and rigor, but its lecture-based, topic-siloed model is not what we are trying to build.

### Summit Ministries: The Comparative Religion Classroom

Summit's "Understanding the Times" curriculum is laser-focused on equipping students to understand and refute the five major competing worldviews: Islam, Secularism, Marxism, New Spirituality, and Postmodernism. It is a fantastic tool for apologetics and cultural engagement.

However, its primary focus is outward-facing (refuting error) rather than inward-facing (building a comprehensive, integrated Christian system). It teaches you what is wrong with other worldviews, but it is less effective at constructing the full, beautiful architecture of our own.

**Conclusion:** Summit provides an excellent model for comparative analysis, which we will incorporate into "The Unredacted Tour." But its core pedagogical goal is different from ours.

### Francis Schaeffer & "How Should We Then Live?": The Art History Tour

Schaeffer's work is iconic and essential. He was a master at showing the downstream cultural consequences of philosophical ideas, tracing the line from the Renaissance to the modern era. His use of art and culture as a diagnostic tool is brilliant.

However, "How Should We Then Live?" is more of a historical and cultural critique than a systematic worldview curriculum. It is a prophetic diagnosis of the West's decline, not a step-by-step guide for building a parallel Christian civilization. It is designed to be watched and contemplated, not necessarily discussed and applied in a small group setting.

**Conclusion:** Schaeffer's work is a vital resource, and we will borrow his method of cultural exegesis. But his model is not the replicable, scalable discipleship engine we need.
