This framework posits that world history will last 6,000 years, followed by a 1,000-year messianic sabbath, based on a creative interpretation of Psalm 90:4 (“a thousand years in your sight are but as a day”).

However, this entire argument collapses when the origin and authority of the two competing calendar systems are examined.

### **2. The Inspired Apostle vs. The Scribal Redaction: The Case of Cainan**

The second major objection is that the inclusion of “Cainan” in the genealogy of Luke 3:36 is a scribal error that Luke mistakenly copied from a flawed LXX. This argument is perhaps the most dangerous of all, as it directly attacks the inspiration of the New Testament itself.

Let us be perfectly clear:

*   **Genesis 11 in the Masoretic Text (MT):** Arphaxad begets Shelah.
*   **Genesis 11 in the Septuagint (LXX):** Arphaxad begets **Cainan**, and Cainan begets Shelah.
*   **Luke 3:36 in the New Testament:** The genealogy of Jesus explicitly includes *“the son of Cainan, the son of Arphaxad.”*

There are only two possibilities here:

1.  The Holy Spirit, inspiring the author of Luke’s Gospel, made a genealogical mistake.
2.  Luke was correct, and he was drawing from a Hebrew source text that, like the LXX, included Cainan.

To preserve the integrity of the New Testament, we must conclude that Luke was correct. This means the MT, which omits Cainan, is the text that has been altered. The LXX and the Gospel of Luke stand together as witnesses to the original, fuller genealogy.

Why was Cainan removed? The Book of Jubilees (also in the Ethiopian Canon and found at Qumran) gives us the answer. Jubilees 11 reveals that Cainan was instrumental in reintroducing the forbidden Watcher knowledge and idolatry after the flood. His removal from the MT serves a clear theological purpose for a post-70 AD rabbinic tradition seeking to sanitize the patriarchal line and distance it from the uncomfortable truths of the cosmic rebellion detailed in 1 Enoch.

The presence of Cainan in Luke is not an error; it is a divine fingerprint, exposing the MT’s redaction and confirming the superior textual witness of the LXX tradition that the Apostles used.

### **3. The Argument from Incredulity: A Failure of Imagination**

The final objection—that men in the post-flood era would not wait over 100 years to have children—is the weakest of all. It is a classic argument from personal incredulity, projecting modern cultural norms and lifespans onto ancient patriarchs who lived for hundreds of years.

*   According to the LXX, Arphaxad lived 535 years.
*   According to the MT, he lived 438 years.

To suggest that a man who will live for over four centuries would not wait until he was 135 to have his heir is not a textual argument; it is a failure of imagination. It assumes that the biological and social realities of the post-flood world were identical to our own. The Scriptures themselves present a world with radically different lifespans, and we must interpret the genealogical data within that revealed context, not our own.
