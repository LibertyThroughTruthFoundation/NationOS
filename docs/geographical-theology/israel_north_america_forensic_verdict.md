## PART TWO: THE ARCHAEOLOGICAL CASE — THE ARTIFACTS ARE FRAUDS

The primary physical evidence cited for an ancient Israelite presence in North America consists of three categories of inscribed artifacts. All three have been forensically debunked.

### The Bat Creek Stone (Tennessee, 1889)

The Bat Creek Stone was excavated by John W. Emmert of the Smithsonian Institution and claimed to bear a Paleo-Hebrew inscription. It became a cornerstone of the "Israel in America" argument. In 2004, archaeologists Robert Mainfort and Mary Kwas published a definitive forensic analysis in *American Antiquity* proving that the inscription was copied directly from an illustration in a Masonic treatise. The Ohio History Connection subsequently published a corroborating analysis. The stone is now classified as a 19th-century hoax by mainstream archaeology, the Tennessee Division of Archaeology, and the Smithsonian Institution itself.

### The Newark Holy Stones (Ohio, 1860)

David Wyrick claimed to have found a series of stones inscribed with Hebrew text in Licking County, Ohio. The Ohio History Connection published its verdict in 2014: "Conclusive Proof That the Newark Decalogue Stone Is a Forgery." The Hebrew used on the stones is modern, not ancient. The letter forms, vocabulary, and grammatical constructions are inconsistent with any pre-Exilic Hebrew writing. The stones were created to support the then-fashionable "Mound Builder" mythology.

### The Los Lunas Decalogue Stone (New Mexico)

A large boulder near Los Lunas, New Mexico bears an inscription claimed to be the Ten Commandments in Paleo-Hebrew. Wikipedia classifies it as a hoax. The inscription has no archaeological context — it is a surface find with no stratigraphic record. The text mixes Hebrew and Greek scripts in a manner that is anachronistically impossible for any genuine ancient inscription. Most scholars date it to the 19th or early 20th century.

### The Pattern

All three major "Hebrew in North America" artifacts are 19th-century productions. They were created during the era of the "Mound Builder" mythology — a period when American intellectual culture was actively constructing a narrative of pre-Columbian "advanced civilization" in North America. This mythology has been thoroughly documented as rooted in racial ideology: the premise that Native Americans were "too primitive" to have built the earthworks, and that a superior "lost white race" — often identified as Israelites — must have preceded them.

Jason Colavito's 2020 book *The Mound Builder Myth: Fake History and the Hunt for a "Lost White Race"* documents this genealogy with 47 scholarly citations. A 2026 paper in *History and Philosophy of the Life Sciences* (Perkins) confirms the consensus: the Mound Builder mythology was debunked by Cyrus Thomas of the Bureau of American Ethnology in the 1890s through systematic excavation proving Native American construction.

## PART THREE: THE GENETIC CASE — DNA DOES NOT LIE

Native American mitochondrial DNA traces to East Asian and Siberian origins through five primary haplogroups: A, B, C, D, and X. Haplogroup X has been cited by proponents of the "Israel in America" thesis as evidence of Middle Eastern ancestry. This claim does not survive scrutiny.

Haplogroup X is found across Eurasia — in Europe, the Middle East, Central Asia, and Siberia. The North American variant (X2a) arrived in the Americas approximately 15,000 years ago, long before any Israelite period. The genetic evidence for this migration is documented in a 2021 *Nature* paper (Willerslev and Meltzer, "Peopling of the Americas as inferred from ancient genomics," 159 citations) which traces all Native American ancestry to East Asian/Siberian populations with no evidence of subsequent gene flow from the ancient Near East.

The most thorough genetic refutation of the "Israelites in America" claim was produced in the context of Book of Mormon apologetics — the LDS church commissioned multiple studies, all of which concluded that "current genetic evidence suggests that Native Americans have a genetic history representative of Asia and not the Middle East" (Thomas Murphy, "Lamanite Genesis, Genealogy, and Genetics," 2002, 43 citations). The LDS church's own genetic research demolished the claim.
